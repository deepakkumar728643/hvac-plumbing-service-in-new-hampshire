How to Winterize Your Plumbing in New Hampshire

📅 December 2024 ✍️ By Plumbing HVAC Services NH ⏱️ 5 min read 🏷️ Winter Tips · Pipe Protection

New Hampshire winters are no joke. With temperatures regularly dropping below 0°F across the state — and wind chills making it feel even colder — unprotected plumbing pipes are at serious risk. A single burst pipe in your NH home can release hundreds of gallons of water and cause tens of thousands of dollars in damage.

The good news: winterizing your NH home's plumbing is straightforward if you know what to do. This guide from our licensed NH plumbers covers everything you need to protect your pipes through even the harshest Granite State winters.

Why NH Pipes Freeze (And Where)

Water expands by about 9% when it freezes. This expansion creates enormous pressure inside pipes — enough to split copper, PVC, and steel pipes. The most vulnerable locations in NH homes include:

  • Pipes in unheated crawl spaces and basements
  • Pipes in exterior walls (especially north-facing walls)
  • Pipes in unheated garages or outbuildings
  • Outdoor hose bibs and irrigation lines
  • Attic pipes in inadequately insulated spaces
  • Vacation and seasonal homes left unoccupied

Step-by-Step NH Plumbing Winterization Checklist

1. Insulate Exposed Pipes

Pipe insulation foam is inexpensive and available at any NH hardware store. Wrap all pipes in unheated spaces — basement, crawl space, garage, and attic — with foam pipe insulation. Pay extra attention to pipes near exterior walls and in areas with drafts.

💡 NH Pro Tip: Heat tape (electric pipe heating cables) is worth the investment for pipes in your most vulnerable NH locations like unheated garages or crawl spaces with poor insulation.

2. Disconnect and Drain Outdoor Hose Bibs

This is the step most NH homeowners forget. Disconnect all garden hoses before the first hard freeze — a connected hose traps water in the spigot, which will freeze and burst. Then shut off the interior valve that feeds your outdoor bibs and open the outdoor spigot to drain remaining water.

3. Drain Your Irrigation System

Irrigation systems left with water in the lines will freeze and crack over NH winters. Most NH homeowners schedule professional blow-out service in October. This involves connecting a compressor to the system and clearing all water from every line and head.

4. Keep Your Heat On — Even When Away

This is critical for NH vacation homes and rental properties. Never let your home drop below 55°F — even if you'll be away for weeks. The cost of heating far outweighs burst pipe repairs and water damage remediation.

⚠️ Warning: Many NH insurance companies may deny water damage claims if the home was unheated or improperly winterized. Check your homeowner's policy.

5. Open Cabinet Doors Under Sinks

Kitchen and bathroom sinks on exterior walls are vulnerable. During extreme cold snaps (-10°F or below), open the cabinet doors to allow warm air to circulate around the supply lines under the sink.

6. Let Faucets Drip in Extreme Cold

During the coldest NH nights, allow faucets served by vulnerable pipes to drip slowly — even a trickle keeps water moving, which greatly reduces freeze risk. Focus on faucets fed by pipes on exterior walls.

7. Know Where Your Main Water Shutoff Is

Every NH homeowner should know exactly where their main water shutoff valve is located. In an emergency, you need to shut off water immediately to limit damage. Locate yours now — before you need it.

8. For Vacant NH Homes: Full System Winterization

For NH vacation homes, seasonal camps, or properties that will be empty all winter, full system winterization is recommended. This involves:

  • Shutting off the main water supply
  • Draining all water from pipes using compressed air
  • Adding RV antifreeze to toilet bowls, tanks, and drain traps
  • Draining the water heater
  • Draining the boiler if applicable

When to Call a Professional NH Plumber

While much of the above can be DIY, some winterization tasks are best handled by a licensed NH plumber:

  • Full vacation home winterization and de-winterization
  • Installing additional insulation in crawl spaces
  • Relocating vulnerable pipes away from exterior walls
  • Installing frost-free outdoor hose bibs
  • Any concerns about your heating system's ability to keep the home warm

What to Do If a Pipe Freezes in Your NH Home

If you turn on a faucet and nothing comes out, you may have a frozen pipe. Do NOT ignore it — frozen pipes can burst. Here's what to do:

  • Open the faucet — this relieves pressure as ice melts
  • Apply gentle heat using a hair dryer or heating pad — never an open flame
  • Start warming from the faucet end toward the frozen section
  • Check for cracks or splits as you thaw — have your shutoff ready
  • If you can't find or access the frozen section, call us immediately
